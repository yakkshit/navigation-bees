1.1 PATH INTEGRATION 
The world is dominated by visual sensations. These visual cues are us ed by animals 
to understand their location (Brines and Gould, 1982; Greif et al., 2014). Orientating in 
animals is fundamental to survival, underscoring the importance of effective strategies 
for navigating the environment (Heinze et al., 2018). Some animals, including insects, 
rodents, and humans, have the ability  to continuously track their position relat ive to a 
defined starting point  (Heinze et al., 2018; Savelli and Knierim, 2019) . They then 
integrate the information about the directions and distances they have traveled with 
the help of external landmarks to transf orm it into a sense of location (Heinze et al., 
2018; Savelli and Knierim, 2019) . Therefore, the transformation of this information 
results in a most direct “home vector” which directly points bac k to the initial starting 
point (Heinze et al., 2018; Patel et al., 2022) , as shown as in Figure 1 (Egelhaaf and 
Lindemann, 2025) . The animal’ s previous experience with this home path is not 
relevant. This vector-based navigation is called path integration (Patel et al., 2024).

1.2.1 Unpolarized light  
Unpolarized light does not exhibit a consistent electric field orientation over time  
(Foster et al., 2018). While each individual wave within the beam still oscillates in only 
one single direction, the beam as a whole consist of many waves with a uniform 
distribution (Foster et al., 2018). The light is considered completely unpolarized, when 
all three polarization-related Stokes parameters are equal, as they describe the state 
and the total intensity of polarization (Born and Wolf, 1999; Kozlov et al., 2018) . It 
further implies the absence of any net  or dominant polarization state (Kozlov et al., 
2018; Kroh et al., 2020).

perpendicular to the direc tion of the light’s propagation (Manion and Stokkermans, 
2025). Thus, the electric field vector or the axis of the electrical field of the wave will 
be constant (Cronin, 2014). Depending on the angle t he electric field vector makes,

1.3 POLARIZED-LIGHT PATTERNS IN THE SKY WITH SUNLIGHT 
Skylight polarization patterns, a naturally occurring phenomenon caused by scattering 
of sunlight in the atmosphere, have been shown to serve as navigation cues for certain 
species (Homberg et al., 2011; Horváth, 2014; Wehner, 2001).  In Guan’s study, they 
argue that t he patterns provide  directional information  (Guan et al., 2018) . 
Furthermore, they suggest that t he information it provides pertains to the solar 
meridian, the neutral point, and the polari zation angle. The solar meridian is defined

as an imaginary line through the sun’s position and the zenith  (Muller et al., 2017) . 
Thus, it divides the celestial sphere into eastern and western regions (Tate, 2010). The 
neutral point lacks emission of polarized light in specific areas (Horváth, 2004; Horváth 
et al., 2002) . Consequently, the position  of the sun , or the solar azimuth,  could be 
detected by the animal and utilized for navigational purposes (Foster et al., 2018). 
Important for its definition, the degree of polarization (DOP) and the angle of 
polarization (AOP) have to be explained. AOP refers to  the axis along which the 
predominant proportion of a light wave’s electric field strength is oriented (Foster et al., 
2019), whereas the DOP is the percentage of polarized light int ensity in total light 
intensity (Kong et al., 2023). 
As the Figure 4 shows, the DOP of skylight exhi bits a minimum close to the sun  and 
anti-sun (Pfeiffer et al., 2011) , where the neutral points (Horváth et al., 2002)  with a 
DOP of 0  occurs (Cronin and Marshall, 2011) . A band of maximum degree of 
polarization is observed at angles near 90° from the sun (Cronin and Marshall, 2011). 
Due to the hemispherical shape of the observable sk y, this polarized light is mostly 
observed in the anti-solar half of the sky (Foster, 2024). A gradual rise in the DOP, 
commencing at a proximity to the sun and anti -sun, and culminating at a 90° to the 
sun, is evident (Cronin and Marshall, 2011).

In the condition of a very clear sky with bright sunlight and an absence of clouds (Figure 
5), the DOP of the band at angles near 90° from the  sun could approach 90 per cent

In the instance that the sun is positioned in close proximity to the horizon, as is the 
case during the dim light of dawn and dusk, the maximum of the DOP of the polarization 
pattern of scattered light from celestial sources is reached (Araújo et al., 2024).

1.3.2 Cloudy sky 
In the presence of clouds,  an increased quantity of suspended particles is present in 
the atmosphere (Isokääntä et al., 2022; Weigelt et al., 2009). Consequently, the effect 
of multiple-path scattering is amplified, resulting in a depolarization of the light (Cronin 
and Marshall, 2011). The DOP decreases (Wang et al., 2016), as shown in the three

different cloudy conditions in Figure 6. A similar effect is also observed for air pollution 
in the lower atmosphere (Cho et al., 2021).  
In addition, the findings of Aycock et al.’s experiment demonstrated, that the precision 
of polarization azimuth is reduced in overcast conditions relative to clear skies (Kong 
et al., 2023) . In conditions of cloud cover, the DOP reac hes approximately 40% at  
angles of 90° close to the sun (Können et al., 1985). 
Nevertheless, given the extensive array of cloud types and the numerous factors that 
influence the DOP of cloud light, the DOP may exhibit variability among different cloud 
formations (Pomozi et al., 2001). 
Nonetheless, should there be regions of the sky that remain uncovered  by clouds, 
animals that navigate using polarization pattern cues may be able to utili ze these 
patches of visible sky (Cronin and Marshall, 2011). The minimum DOP, that is essential 
for ensuring accurate navigation within a specific patch of skylight is determined to be 
approximately 5-10% (Pomozi et al., 2001).

atmosphere, which leads to an increase in the degree of polarization and the precision 
of polarization cues. While the degree of polarization can vary with the diversity of 
cloud types, small clear patches of the sky can still provide enough information about 
the polarization for accurate navigation.

1.4 DORSAL RIM AREA – POLARIZATION SENSITIVITY IN BUMBLEBEES 
The capacity of animals to utilize polarization pattern cues of the skylight for navigation 
is dependent on the presence of specialized  visual s ystems and receptors , which 
facilitate the detection and processing of this information (Greif et al., 2014; Ma et al., 
2015; Wehner, 2001). 
In the eyes of bumblebees, honey bees, locusts, desert ants, fruit fries and many other 
insect species  (Labhart and Meyer, 1999) , a specific eye  region is essential for 
polarization sensitivity (Hardcastle et al.; Labhart and Meyer, 1999; Zittrell et al., 2020).  
The rhabdoms are photosensitive structures located in the eyes of many invertebrates 
(Grusch et al., 1997; Reisenman et al., 2002) . The rhabdoms contain photosensitive 
cells that are characterized by their dense microvilli  (Cronin, 2014; Rutherford and 
Horridge, 1965) , which play a fundamental role for light absorption and 
phototransduction (Eguchi et al., 1962; Rutherford and Horridge, 1965) . The specific 
configuration of these microvilli determines a certain function of the rhabdoms (Foster, 
2024; Greiner et al., 2007; Matsushita et al., 2012). In the dorsal rim area (DRA) of the 
compound eye (Araújo et al., 2024; Foster, 2024), the rhabdoms play a crucial role for 
polarization sensitivity (Foster, 2024; Greiner et al., 2007; Karman et al., 2012) . The 
DRA is positioned in the upper portion of the compound eye and exhibits a dist inct 
structural configuration compared to regions in the compound eye outside the DRA 
(Jie et al., 2023). The DRA is lighter in color compared to the surrounding regions of 
the eye, noticeable as a grey area within the compound eye, which, as shown in Figure 
7 (Labhart and Meyer, 1999). This gray coloring is associated with its ability to disperse 
light (Foster, 2024).

Furthermore, the direction of the provided pola rization stimulus is important, as it is 
shown in Figure 13 and Figure 14. In the LR condition, the fictive nest, indicated by the 
vertical direction of the stimulus, correlates with their home.  Thus, the bumblebees 
exhibit a higher tendency to go back to the fictive nest. As it is the same case in weaker 
condition, the bumblebees are not necessary able to use the provided stimulus and 
therefore the use of another potential information for orientation could be possible. The 
bumblebees could receive this information through the  usage of their long -term 
memory. 
To summarize, the bumblebees still orient toward the home direction in the TB and 
even under the conflicting LR condition. In some trials, the bumblebees are reacting to 
the stimulus, but some of the bumblebees doesn’t seem to react to the direction of the 
polarization at all. Although the data of the training trails indicated that the bumblebees 
are reacting to the stimulus, a higher concentrated clustering of the bearings closer to 
0° and 180° were expected. With higher degree of polarization,  it was predicted that 
the bumblebees were strongly reacting to the stimulus and moved towards the true or 
fictive nest, as a higher degree of polarization offers a stronger and more reliable cue. 
Other than expected, the bumblebees orientation towards their real home seems to be 
better in weaker TB conditions.  
This result could suggest that the bumblebees are not performing their path integration 
with the  only use of polarization. This behavior would be important, if the skylight 
polarization patterns would not b e available or less available in the bumblebee’ s 
natural environment as it would be the case in cloudy or foggy conditions. Furthermore, 
the bumblebees could learn to find their way back to their home with using their 
knowledge to walk back, which indicates the usage of their long -term memory. Either 
way, as the Rayleigh test was not significant in all of the observed conditions, a larger 
sample of the different trials is obligatory to get clear evidence.
